Limits

Small sample size which affects the reliability of your conclusions. Lack of participant diversity, limiting how broadly the results can be applied. Puzzle difficulty may have been too easy or too hard, influencing performance. External factors like distractions or different environments impacting the results. Time constraints limited data collection and analysis. Self-reporting errors if participants recorded their own times. Varying chess skill levels among participants, affecting comparisons. Puzzle selection may not perfectly represent the problem-solving skills gained from chess.

Conclusion

In this study, I compared the problem-solving abilities of chess players and non-chess players using logic grid puzzles. The results suggest that chess players , on average, solved the puzzles more quickly than non-chess players, indicating a potential link between chess experience and enhanced problem-solving skills. However, given the small sample size and the potential impact of external factors such as distractions and differing environments, these results should be interpreted with caution. While the data showed some clear trends, further research with a larger and more diverse sample would be needed to confirm the findings. Additionally, using a broader range of puzzle types could provide a more complete picture of the cognitive benefits associated with playing chess. Despite these limitations, the project highlights how strategic games like chess may contribute to improved logical thinking and cognitive skills. In future studies, it would be valuable to explore whether different levels of chess experience yield varying effects on problem-solving ability, and to consider other cognitive challenges that may be even more strongly influenced by chess play.
